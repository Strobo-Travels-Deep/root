One-Sentence Claim

Hasse et al. phase-lock four oscillators (MW reference, optical running-wave phase, spin, motion) and use a stroboscopic Raman pulse train on a short-period travelling polarisation gradient (λeff ≈ 140 nm, ηLF ≈ 0.40) to map an ion's motional phase-space dynamics with super-resolution, where position appears primarily as a spin-fringe phase shift and the p-quadrature proxy appears primarily as a nonlinear contrast modulation from finite-pulse-time averaging.

Glossary

Momentum observable
A velocity / p-quadrature proxy estimator inferred from stroboscopic phase+contrast response, not a direct canonical P measurement.
Super-resolution
Position sensitivity at the scale of an engineered short-period polarisation gradient (λeff ≈ 140 nm ≪ optical wavelength). Does not automatically imply sub-x0 resolution or QND.
Sideband scaling ~ηs
Heuristic mnemonic. Actual matrix elements depend on occupation number n through Laguerre polynomial structure; quoted values are rough estimates, not quantitative bounds.

Evidence Summary

Experimentally established

Four-oscillator phase lock (MW reference, optical running-wave, spin, motion). Stroboscopic pulse train on AC Raman travelling wave: λeff ≈ 140 nm, Ω/(2π) ≈ 0.3 MHz. Lamb–Dicke parameters: ηLF ≈ 0.40, ηMF ≈ 0.23, ηHF ≈ 0.18 (AC); η ≈ 0 (MW, CC). Pulse-train timing: tπ/2,S ≈ 30 × 770 ns ≈ 23.1 μs. Decoder functions obtained numerically via QuTiP.

Analytically grounded

Coherent-state expectation: ⟨α|exp(iη(a+a†))|α⟩ = exp(−η²/2)·exp(i2η Re α). Clean Debye–Waller factor plus phase proportional to displacement quadrature — the zeroth-order semiclassical baseline. Relying on numerical calibration alone

Full decoder map (phase, contrast) → (x, p-proxy) for arbitrary states. Backaction assessment beyond mean quanta. Behaviour for non-Gaussian inputs.

Strategic Resolution

Primary framing: Tomography. The η ≈ 0.4 nonlinearity of the exponential coupling exp(iη(a+a†)) is a structural asset for characteristic function sampling (access to higher harmonics) but a liability for strict BAE (which requires linear quadrature isolation). The Cartographer's argument prevails; Guardian concurs; Scout is neutral.

Guardian proviso (preserved): Backaction quantification remains the first analytical task regardless of framing. For tomography, the question becomes: "Can we bound backaction tightly enough to certify reconstruction fidelity?" — more tractable and more honest than the BAE question.

L6 reclassification: Downgraded from Inconsistent to Underdetermined (supervisor correction). A weak-coupling / short-flash / small-excursion regime may make the exponential interaction effectively linear over occupied phase-space. Open empirical question, not logically excluded.

WP-D charter: Evaluates BAE feasibility only. Does not attempt to "rescue" BAE at η ≈ 0.4 with exponential coupling. If L6 discriminant fails for current apparatus, result archived as resolved.

Interaction Hamiltonian

For one motional mode the full interaction Hamiltonian (from the paper's Supplement) reads:

HTI = ℏ(ωz/2)σz + ℏωm a†a + ℏ(Ω/2)[C(η,a,a†)σ− + C†(η,a,a†)σ+]

where C(η,a,a†) = exp(iη(a+a†)). The coupling is an exponential in the position quadrature, not truncated at first order. This is the structural origin of both the method's power (access to higher sidebands, nonlinear phase-space mapping) and its complications (backaction, decoder nonlinearity).

Where η ≈ 0.4 Enters

For the antiparallel (AC) Raman travelling wave: λeff ≈ 140 nm, ηLF ≈ 0.40, ηMF ≈ 0.23, ηHF ≈ 0.18. MW and co-propagating Raman (CC) are effectively η ≈ 0. The system is not in the deep Lamb–Dicke regime; truncating C at linear order is generally not justified for higher sidebands or large coherent amplitudes.

Sideband content from exponential coupling

In the Fock basis, matrix elements ⟨n′|C|n⟩ involve Laguerre polynomials. With η = 0.4, the Debye–Waller factor exp(−η²/2) ≈ 0.92 is close to unity; sideband amplitudes scale roughly as ηs (heuristic): ~0.4 for first, ~0.16 for second, ~0.064 for third order. Higher orders are non-negligible, especially when Ω is not extremely small, pulses are short/broadband, or the motional state is displaced.

Stroboscopic Pulse-Train Mechanism

Geometry: phase-space → spin readout

The travelling wave imprints a spin-dependent phase proportional to the ion's instantaneous position along keff. For an idealised instantaneous pulse: φmeas(t) ~ keff x(t) + φ0. With NS flashes per motional cycle and controlled phase stepping, the full dynamics are sampled stroboscopically.

Why the p-proxy appears as contrast, not just phase

During a flash of duration δt, the ion moves by Δx ~ v·δt. This causes averaging of exp(ik x(t)) over the flash, reducing fringe contrast. Hence: position → mainly a phase shift of the spin fringe; velocity → mainly a nonlinear contrast modulation (larger v sweeps through more optical phase during δt). The decoder fits phase and contrast vs. analysis phase φ, then inverts via numerical calibration.

Semiclassical Baseline

The coherent-state expectation of the displacement operator provides the clean zeroth-order reference:

⟨α|exp(iη(a+a†))|α⟩ = exp(−η²/2) · exp(i2η Re α)

A Debye–Waller factor and a phase proportional to the coherent displacement quadrature. Deviations from this baseline quantify backaction and nonclassicality.

Backaction Structure

The paper evaluates backaction via δ⟨n⟩ = ⟨n⟩fin − ⟨n⟩ini and notes the final state is "non-trivial, but near-coherent." True BAE requires coupling to a single rotating quadrature; the exponential operator at η ≈ 0.4 intrinsically couples both quadratures. Whether a perturbative regime exists where the interaction is effectively linear (and thus approximately BAE) is an open question with a well-defined discriminant — see L6 in the Ledger.

Tomographic Potential

Measuring ⟨exp(ik x)⟩ for various phases and timings is essentially sampling the characteristic function of the motional state. With phase control, one can access different quadratures and reconstruct quasi-probability distributions (Wigner, Q-function). The η ≈ 0.4 regime grants stronger sensitivity and access to higher harmonics — but also stronger measurement backaction and more complicated inversion.

Practical Starting Points

1. Analytic warm-up: coherent-state signal

Begin with the cleanest calculation available: compute ⟨α|exp(iη(a+a†))|α⟩ for a coherent state |α⟩ evolving as α(t) = |α|exp(−iωt). This gives the zeroth-order signal (phase shift ∝ Re α, Debye–Waller contrast). Then introduce finite flash duration δt by averaging the phase factor over the flash:

⟨exp(ikx(t))⟩flash ≈ (1/δt) ∫ exp(ik x₀ cos(ωt + θ)) dt

For small ωδt, expand x(t) linearly → sinc-like contrast vs. velocity. This is the natural entry point for WP-A.1.

2. Numbers to reproduce first

Using the paper's parameters (Ω/(2π) ≈ 0.3 MHz, ηLF ≈ 0.40, ωLF/(2π) ≈ 1.3 MHz, NS ≈ 30): estimate keffΔx during a flash for |α| = 1, 5, 10. Then estimate keff·v vs. Ω to resolve the geometric-averaging-vs.-Doppler question (R5). These are order-of-magnitude checks that anchor all subsequent work.

3. Simulation scaffold for WP-C

Use QuTiP to construct the measurement map M: ρ → (φ, contrast) for displaced thermal, Fock, squeezed, and cat states at the paper's parameters. Vary the analysis phase φ across [0, 2π) and record the spin-flip probability. Then ask: can you uniquely recover ρ from this data? If not — which additional settings (variable δt, multiple η, phase-space rotations) restore injectivity?

4. Backaction bookkeeping

For each simulated measurement in WP-C, track the full certification set: purity, all four quadrature disturbance quantities, and coherent-state overlap. This data feeds directly into both L5 resolution and the tomographic fidelity assessment. Start with coherent states (where you have the analytic baseline from WP-A.1) before moving to non-Gaussian states.
